How can I involve?

You want to help? Follow these easy steps and make the difference!

  • 1. Support petitions to make a difference!

    Create or sign a petition in www.change.org for Gender Equality. Share with friends and raise awareness about this issue. Lets more people know about Global Goal number 5!

  • 2. Educate the girls!

    Support schools that educate young girls. If girls are educated, they have options that might include employment, empowerment and self-sufficiency. Education is one means to break the cycle of dependence and abuse.

  • 3. Health care system for women!

    Improve the health care system to be prepared to address women’s health issues, including the trauma of abuse. Medical and mental health specialists must be educated about making referrals to women’s shelters, safe houses and support services.

  • 4. Develop child's culture!

    Start early in a child’s developmental process with cultural interventions that stress equality, respect and options in family lifestyles. For instance, if boys are socialized at home to respect women and girls, they will be less likely to grow into abusers. If girls are educated at home about cultural and societal options and self-empowerment, they might begin to think and “act as if” gender equality existed.

Maternal mortality rate:

When asked to estimate the rate of maternal deaths in their country, only 6% of policymakers came within 20% of the most recently available figure.

Early marriage rate:

On the rates of early marriage for girls, just a quarter of policymakers came within 20% of the most recently available figure.

Share of women in the labour force:

When estimating the share of women in the labour force, 31% of policymakers came within 20% of the most recently available figure.

The priority of Gender Equality

When combining response categories "high" and "very high" priority education and health stand out as being most-often cited as priorities (81% and 67% respectively).

Attention given to gender equality

There is a considerable difference in the responses given by women and men. Two in three women support the statement that there is "too little attention" to gender issues in policymaking, whereas just one in three men felt the same way.
